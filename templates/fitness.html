PHYSICAL FITNESS

Being overweight, especially around your middle, increases your risk of heart disease. Excess weight can lead to conditions that increase your chances of developing heart disease, including high blood pressure, high cholesterol and Type 2 diabetes.

One way to see if your weight is healthy is to calculate your body mass index, or BMI, which uses your height and weight to determine whether you have a healthy or unhealthy percentage of body fat. A BMI of 25 or higher is considered overweight, and is generally associated with higher cholesterol, higher blood pressure, and an increased risk of heart disease and stroke. Waist circumference also can be a useful tool to measure how much abdominal fat you have. Your risk of heart disease is higher if your waist measurement is greater than: 1. 40 inches or 101.6 centimeters for men. 2. 35 inches or 88.9 centimeters for women. Even a small weight loss can be beneficial. Reducing your weight by just 3% to 5% can decrease triglycerides, which are fats in your blood, and blood sugar, or glucose. This decrease also can reduce your risk of Type 2 diabetes. Losing even more weight lowers your blood pressure and blood cholesterol level.

1. Blood pressure —

Regular blood pressure screenings usually start in childhood. Starting at 18, your blood pressure should be measured at least once every two years to screen for high blood pressure as a risk factor for heart disease and stroke. If you're between 18 and 39, and have risk factors for high blood pressure, you'll likely be screened once per year. People 40 and older also are given a blood pressure test annually.

2. Cholesterol levels —

Adults generally have their cholesterol measured at least once every four to six years. Cholesterol screening usually starts at 20, though earlier testing may be recommended if you have other risk factors, such as a family history of early-onset heart disease.



3. Type 2 diabetes screening —

Diabetes is a risk factor for heart disease. If you have risk factors for diabetes, such as being overweight or having a family history of diabetes, your health care provider may recommend early screening. If your weight is normal and you don't have other risk factors for Type 2 diabetes, screening is recommended, beginning at 45, with retesting every three years.

If you have a condition such as high cholesterol, high blood pressure or diabetes, your health care provider may prescribe medications and recommend lifestyle changes. Make sure that you take your medications as your health care provider prescribes and follow a healthy lifestyle plan.
